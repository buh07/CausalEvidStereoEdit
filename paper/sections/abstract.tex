As language models scaled to internet data, exhaustive curation became infeasible and stereotype learning became an expected side effect of training. A common response is post-training local editing: identify an internal direction linked to harmful behavior and try to remove it. However, the causal effectiveness of this method is unclear. In this paper, we present a causal audit of local stereotype editing across seven open-weight LLMs using matched inject-versus-remove interventions, component-level diagnostics, and cross-source transfer stress tests. The main result is asymmetric controllability: stereotype preference is easier to increase than to reliably decrease. In matched tests, score-level inject-minus-remove asymmetry is significant in 5/7 models, and margin-level asymmetry is significant in 7/7. Supporting diagnostics show model-dependent sign reliability, suppressor contamination in ranked intervention sets, and no reliable large-effect cross-source transfer under current settings. Overall, localization provides control leverage but not a debiasing guarantee; deployment-facing claims should be validated with direct causal intervention and transfer stress tests.
