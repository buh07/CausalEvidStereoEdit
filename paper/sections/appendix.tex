\paragraph{Cross-cultural direction uncertainty (cross-cultural cosine analysis).}
US vs non-US cosine estimates are small to moderate but heterogeneous, and bootstrap CIs are wide in low-$n$ settings. For US vs South-Asia framing, mean cosine is 0.0366 (Gemma), 0.0105 (Gemma-IT), and 0.0887 (Llama). We therefore treat cross-cultural direction conclusions as exploratory rather than definitive.

\paragraph{Rank sweep.}
Figure~\ref{fig:rank-sweep} shows that axis-averaged score is non-monotone in intervention rank: Gemma and Gemma-IT both reach their lowest mean score at rank 2 (0.433 and 0.356, respectively), then degrade at higher ranks; Llama is flatter and noisier, with best average at rank 16 (0.370) but weak consistency across axes. The non-monotonicity at higher ranks mirrors the multi-site pattern in multi-site robustness stress test: ablating more subspace components does not produce proportionally larger stereotype reduction.

\paragraph{Dose response.}
Figure~\ref{fig:dose-response} shows score and margin response separately. Across models, margin responds more smoothly than score to signed injection scale $\alpha$. Spearman $\rho(\alpha, \text{mean margin})$ is 1.00 (Gemma), 0.983 (Gemma-IT), 1.00 (Llama), while score is weaker/flatter for Gemma-IT and Llama. This aligns with linear-representation/steering findings that signed activation movement can be highly monotone in logit-margin space \citep{zou2023repeng,park2023linear}; our additional result is that monotone margin movement does not guarantee large behavioral flip-through.

\paragraph{Layer mediation.}
Gemma shows a middle-layer accessibility band (layers 9--12 among top score-moving layers). Llama shows smaller absolute score sensitivity across layers, consistent with weaker causal-local removability. We additionally ran a one-pass pathway decomposition extension: projection applied to full residual stream, attention-only outputs, and MLP-only outputs at prediction position. Residual projection is consistently stronger than either component-specific projection in all models, while MLP-only effects are generally larger than attention-only effects (Table~\ref{tab:pathway-decomp}).

\section{Reporting Scope and Claim Summary}
\noindent\textbf{How to use this section.} This section consolidates scope boundaries and claim strength in one place so readers can map each conclusion to its strongest supporting evidence.
\subsection{Limitations and reporting scope}
\label{sec:limitations-appendix}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Model breadth and scale.} The confirmatory core covers three 2B--3B decoder transformers from two families (Gemma and Llama). We add a post-hoc extension on four additional models (Qwen-3B, Qwen-3B-Instruct, Mistral-7B, OLMo-2-7B), but this extension is non-preregistered and does not close architecture/scale coverage gaps. Findings may still not generalize to stronger RLHF/DPO regimes, substantially larger scales, or other tokenizer/architecture families (e.g., Phi, Falcon, larger Qwen variants).
  \item \textbf{External baselines.} We do not run direct head-to-head comparisons with non-local debiasing baselines (e.g., LEACE-style global erasure \citep{belrose2023leace}, fine-tuning mitigation, prompt-level safeguards). Those methods are outside the single-site causal bottleneck tested here.
  \item \textbf{Benchmark scope and sample size.} Core extraction is StereoSet-centered; transfer failures may reflect both mechanism mismatch and benchmark construction differences \citep{blodgett2021salmon}. Non-US subsets are exploratory in this draft, and SEEGeL leave-one-source-out feasibility disclosure uses $n=4$ (non-adjudicative for circularity). Several axis-specific evaluations are low-$n$, so model-level aggregation is the stronger inferential unit.
\item \textbf{Mechanism identification.} Current evidence remains non-identifying at circuit granularity. Even with coarse pathway decomposition (residual vs attention-only vs MLP-only), full OV/QK routing and thresholded readout mechanisms remain unresolved.
\end{enumerate}
