Easy to Add, Hard to Erase: Causal Evidence on Stereotype Editing in
LLMs

Anonymous ACL submission

001

Abstract

002

Can stereotypes in language models be surgically removed once we locate them? We answer this by auditing stereotype-editing methods with causal interventions across three openweight LLMs. We evaluate four method families: residual direction editing, attributionranked single-site ablation, multi-site ablation,
and cross-dataset target transfer. Injection is
significant in all three tested models, while the
direct inject-minus-remove asymmetry contrast
is significant in two (Gemma-2-2B and Llama3.2-3B) and directionally consistent but nonsignificant in Gemma-2-2B-IT after FDR correction. Injection specificity against shuffledaxis controls is significant in Gemma-IT and
Llama and trend-level in Gemma. We identify
three recurring failure patterns: sign-blind intervention sets can backfire, attribution signs
are often unreliable guides to causal direction,
and overlapping ranked components across
benchmarks do not reliably yield large crosssource transfer at current sample sizes. We
report both behavioral effects (stereotype score
delta) and mechanistic effects (margin delta)
to distinguish decision flips from confidence
shifts. Overall, the evidence is consistent with
distributed or redundant support for stereotype behavior in these models, while remaining
non-identifying mechanistically; consequently,
debiasing claims require direct causal validation and transfer stress testing, not localization
alone.

and many mitigation methods assume that once
stereotype-related signal is localized, local editing

This paper audits that assumption as a mechanistic question: when stereotype-editing methods

Gemma-2-2B-IT, Llama-3.2-3B): residual direction editing, attribution-ranked single-site editing,
